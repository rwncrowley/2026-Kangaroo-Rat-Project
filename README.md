# RC-2026-Kangaroo-Rat-Project

## Background:
This data is the data collected from Rowan Crowley's kangaroo rat senior thesis project. Small mammal trapping at Hastings Natural History Reservation and Fort Ord Natural Reserve presented two species of kangaroo rats, the Narrow-faced kangaroo rat Dipodomys Venustus and the Heerman's kangaroo rat Dipodyms heermanni respectively. 

These two locations have varying ecosystem structures. Fort Ord Natural Reserve has soil that is entirely loose sand dunes and includes maritime chapparal, grasslands, oak savannah and oak woodland. Hastings Natural History reservation has sandy loam soil, which includes more clay which makes the soil more dense. It's habitats include grasslands, Chamise chapparal and oak woodlands. These species have different habitat preferences the Dipodomys Heermanni prefering grasslands and the Dipodomys Venustus prefer the chapparal. After small mammal trapping at these sites only dipodomys heermanni have been found and measured from Fort Ord Natural reserve, and only Dipodomys Venustus have been found and measured from Hastings Natural History reservation. This study aims to determine what morphological differences occur between Dipodomys venustus and Dipodyms heermanni. Our hypothesis is that these measurements can be used to differentiate between species and we will analyze the data to test each measurements ability to predict species.


## Hypothesis/Motivating Question:
Can the new and standard field measurements taken be used to differentiate between the species Dipodomys heermanni and Dipodomys Venustus? Which measurements are good or poor predictors of species? The new mesurements taken were Hind limb length, Foot Width, Wrist Width, Finger length, Hand Width, and Toe length. Each was analyzed for their variation between species, and the same was done with the standard measurements taken on small mammals. These included Body length, Tail length, Head length, Foot length, and Ear length.


## Conclusions:
Further research should test the good predictive measurements in more locations, especially where the species are sympatric. We cannot distinguish whether location and/or species are our cause for differential measurements because of our data’s limitations, but we did find some useful trends for future research. More accurately defined species descriptions can help small mammal biologists with field identification, which is much less money than genetic testing.These methods of testing non-standard measurements can be implemented into future research in other taxa to explore new methods of species ID. This study inspires future projects tackling why finger length varies so greatly between species and how much of an effect environmental conditions play on individual morphological measurements?


## Target Audience:
The results of this study can be used by future scientists for field identification of species. These species are very similar and can be difficult to tell apart in the field, sometimes requiring genetic analysis for correct identification, which can be expensive. These species have a lot of overlap in morphometric measurements, and it can be difficult to accurately assign species. These new measurements and defining which measurements work well to define species could help  to assign species descriptions more accurately.
